Why We Survey

Understanding the foundations of emergence in cellular automata

Computational Irreducibility

In 2002, Stephen Wolfram formalized a principle called computational irreducibility: for many computational systems, the only way to determine what they'll do is to run them. There are no shortcuts.

This applies directly to cellular automata. Just because a rule is simple (e.g., "birth on 3 neighbors, survival on 2-3") doesn't mean its behavior is simple. Conway's Game of Life proves this — a trivial rule specification produces Turing-complete complexity.

We can't predict which of the 262,144 Life-like rules will be interesting. We have to run them and measure what happens.

This is why systematic survey matters. Mathematical analysis can tell us some things (e.g., rules with no survival conditions will always die out), but the interesting behaviors — chaos, gliders, computation — emerge only through simulation.

Why Topology Matters

The "universe" a cellular automaton lives in isn't just its rules — it's also the shape of space itself. The same rule running on different topologies can behave completely differently.

Flat Grid

Cells at edges have fewer neighbors. Boundary effects can trap or destroy patterns. Most common in implementations.

Toroidal

Left edge connects to right, top to bottom. No boundaries. Patterns can wrap around. This is what we use for surveys.

Spherical

Like a globe. Cells near poles have different neighbor configurations. How does curvature affect emergence?

Hyperbolic

Negative curvature means more neighbors than Euclidean space. Patterns spread differently. Largely unexplored.

If we want to understand emergence, topology isn't optional — it's fundamental. A glider that works on a torus might not exist on a sphere. A rule that's boring on a flat grid might be fascinating in hyperbolic space.

Hierarchical Emergence

Some cellular automata produce patterns made of patterns. This hierarchy is what makes certain rule spaces "rich" for engineering and discovery.

Level 0
Individual Cells
The atomic units. On or off, alive or dead.
Level 1
Stable Structures
Still lifes (static), oscillators (periodic), gliders (moving). These persist.
Level 2
Pattern Generators
Glider guns emit gliders. Breeders emit guns. Structures that create other structures.
Level 3
Computational Structures
Logic gates, memory, counters. Patterns that compute.
Level 4+
Universal Computation
Turing machines, self-replicators. The rule space becomes a programming language.

Not all rules support all levels. Most rules barely reach Level 1. Understanding what enables each level — how universe structure (rules, topology, tiling) affects hierarchical depth — is our core research question.

Multi-Pass Analysis

As we learn more about the rule space, we ask deeper questions. Our analysis is structured in passes, each narrowing focus and increasing depth.

1

Initial Survey

Run every rule for 1000 steps from random initial conditions. Measure basic metrics: lambda, gamma, dimension, period. Fast, broad coverage.

2

Deeper Analysis

Run expensive computations on promising subsets. Glider detection, structure classification, longer simulations. Filter for rules showing complex behavior.

3

Curated Study

Deep investigation of the most interesting rules. Pattern cataloging, emergence analysis, engineering potential. Human insight combined with computation.

The Atlas lets you explore rules from any pass. As new metrics are developed and new passes run, the database grows richer. Each contribution expands what we can discover.

The Extensible Vision

This isn't a static dataset — it's a living research platform. Anyone can:

Run surveys that append the database. Your browser contributes to the shared understanding.

Write new metrics. Come up with a new way to measure rules? Implement the algorithm, run it, add the metadata.

Filter and analyze at multiple layers. Use coarse metrics to find candidates, then deep analysis to understand them.

Vary the fundamentals. Topology, grid tiling, neighborhood definition — each creates a new universe to explore.

Our goal: understand how the design choices of a universe — its rules, its geometry, its connectivity — affect what kinds of complexity can emerge within it.
